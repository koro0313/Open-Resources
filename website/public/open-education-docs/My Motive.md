# My Motive

Having experienced the education system from both sides—first as a student and now as a teacher too—I have witnessed first-hand how the current system fails everyone. This model is born out of that frustration and a desire to build something that actually supports growth rather than hindering it.

---

## The Student's Perspective: A System Designed to Slow Us Down

As a student, I never really cared about what the school had to teach. Instead of following the standard curriculum, I focused on my passion. Looking back, that choice turned out to be the best decision for my future:
*   I gained invaluable hands-on experience by working on real-world projects during my school days.
*   I am highly valued in my college today specifically because of my practical, real-world skills.

My path and how to pursue it was always clear to me. While my teachers and parents were supportive, it was the system itself that constantly pulled me back. The requirement to spend endless hours studying irrelevant subjects affected and frustrated me the most. Instead of supporting me and my passion, the system acted as a barrier designed to slow me down.

---

## The Teacher's Perspective: Witnessing the Cycle Repeat

Later, when I started giving tuitions (which I have now done for over two years), I witnessed this exact same pattern repeating itself from the other side. As a teacher, I see many potential in my students, yet the system continues to hold them back:
*   **A student who is really good at math**, but is forced to halt their progress to study unrelated subjects just for the exams.
*   **A student who built an electronics project out of curiosity**, but has their creative drive sidelined by rote homework.
*   **A student who is highly interested in social studies (SST)**, but is restricted by rigid curricula.
*   **A student who is great at art**, but has their creative expression undervalued by the system.
*   **A student who has won trophies in sports**, but is constantly told to prioritize memorizing exam answers over athletic development.

I have always encouraged my students to explore their interests, and I want nothing more than to help them dive deeper into what they love. However, the system forces me to do the opposite:
*   I hated when I had to scold them for not memorizing trivial answers about a king who lived 2,000 years ago.
*   I hated when I had to tell a talented athlete to focus on mugging up text for exams.
*   I hated when I had to tell a mathematically brilliant student to stop focusing on math and study other subjects just to pass the upcoming exams.

The system is constantly there—hampering everyone, pulling them back, slowing them down, and actively trying to kill their natural curiosity.

---

## The Root Cause: A Collective Cage

The individuals within the system are not the problem:
*   **Schools** are not the enemy.
*   **Teachers** are not the enemy.
*   **Parents** are not the enemy.
*   **Students** are not the enemy.

We are all smart enough to know what is right and what is wrong for our growth. The real problem is the outdated education system that we all collectively follow. Because we continue to participate in and conform to it, we have caged ourselves inside it. 

The Open Education Model is an attempt to break that cage.

---

## How the Idea of this Model Started

Frustrated by the system all these years, I always wondered what an ideal education system would look like—one that:
*   Gives students the absolute freedom to explore their interests.
*   Supports them rather than trying to pull them back.
*   Prepares them fully for the challenges of the real world.

I used to speak about these ideas with people around me, but blinded by the norms, no one seemed to understand. Filled with doubt, I eventually stopped talking about it.

### A Spark of Confirmation
Then, amid all this chaos, I came to know about **Sonam Wangchuk** and his school. Seeing a real-world school designed specifically for students who had failed in the current system—yet still turned out to be great—assured me that it wasn't just my personal rage blaming the system. The system indeed is broken.

Now, as the nation is beginning to realize this problem and people are repeatedly calling for a better education system, I spent a week compiling all my ideas of what an ideal education system should be. This documentation is the result of that effort.
